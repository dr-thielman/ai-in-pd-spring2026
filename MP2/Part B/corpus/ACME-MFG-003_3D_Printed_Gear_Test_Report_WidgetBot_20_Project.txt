TEST REPORT: 3D PRINTED SPUR GEAR DURABILITY
Project: WidgetBot 2.0 (WB2-TR-008)
Author: Dr. Elena Vasquez, Test Engineering
Date: August 12, 2025

OBJECTIVE
Characterize the fatigue life and failure modes of FDM-printed spur gears for the WidgetBot 2.0 waving mechanism.

SPECIMEN DETAILS
- Gear geometry: Module 1.0, 20-tooth spur gear, 20-degree pressure angle
- Material: FilaTech PolyPro PLA+
- Layer height: 0.20 mm
- Print orientation: gear axis perpendicular to build plate (teeth oriented perpendicular to print layers)
- Infill: 100%
- Face width: 4 mm
- Number of specimens per test condition: 5

TEST CONDITIONS
Constant-torque fatigue testing at room temperature (22 C). Gears were meshed with a hardened steel pinion to isolate PLA gear failure. Three torque levels tested:

TEST RESULTS
Torque Level 1 — 0.15 N-m input:
All 5 specimens survived 50,000+ cycles with no visible damage. Calculated Lewis bending stress at tooth root: 8.2 MPa. Test stopped at 50,000 cycles (target life for WidgetBot).

Torque Level 2 — 0.25 N-m input:
Average life: 12,000 cycles. Range: 8,400 to 16,200 cycles. Calculated Lewis bending stress: 13.7 MPa. All failures were delamination at layer boundaries on the tension side of the tooth root — NOT classical tooth root fracture as predicted by Lewis beam theory.

Torque Level 3 — 0.35 N-m input:
Average life: under 500 cycles. Range: 180 to 720 cycles. Calculated Lewis bending stress: 19.2 MPa. Failure mode identical to Level 2: layer delamination, not root fracture.

ANALYSIS
Comparing the failure stress to the predicted Lewis bending stress, teeth failed at approximately 60% of the stress predicted by classical Lewis theory using bulk PLA properties. This is consistent with the interlayer adhesion strength (28 MPa) being the governing material property rather than the bulk tensile strength (52 MPa).

The dominant failure mode — delamination at layer boundaries — confirms that interlayer bonding is the weak link in printed gears, not the tooth geometry. This has implications for safety factor selection.

RECOMMENDATIONS
1. Use a safety factor of 2.0 for printed gears (vs. typical 1.5 for machined metal gears). This accounts for the interlayer weakness and lot-to-lot material variation.
2. Minimum face width: 4 mm at module 1.0. Our early prototypes with 3 mm face width showed premature failures even at low loads.
3. Wider face width improves life significantly: 6 mm face width gears showed approximately 40% improvement in cycle life at the same torque level compared to 4 mm.
4. Layer height matters: gears printed at 0.15 mm layer height lasted approximately 30% longer than 0.20 mm layer height at the same load, likely due to better interlayer bonding with thinner layers.
5. Always print gears with 100% infill. Reduced infill caused catastrophic tooth failures in preliminary testing.

These findings should inform all future ACME projects using printed gears. The safety factor of 2.0 has been incorporated into ACME's internal gear design guidelines (ACME-ENG-001).
