RIDGELINE ENGINEERING PARTNERS — TOLERANCE ANALYSIS PROCEDURE
Document Number: ENG-TOL-01, Revision 3
Effective Date: February 2024
Author: Angela Cruz, PE, Mechanical Design Group Lead

This procedure defines Ridgeline's standard approach for performing dimensional tolerance analysis on mechanical assemblies.

1. SCOPE
This procedure applies to all mechanical design projects where dimensional variation affects form, fit, or function. Tolerance analysis is mandatory for: (a) assemblies with more than 5 contributing dimensions, (b) assemblies with clearance or interference fits, (c) medical device components subject to design controls, and (d) any assembly where the client specifies a Cpk requirement.

2. ANALYSIS METHODS

2.1 Worst-Case Analysis
Sum of all tolerances at their extreme limits. Guarantees 100% interchangeability but results in overly tight individual tolerances. Required for safety-critical assemblies and when production volumes are below 500 units.

2.2 RSS (Root Sum of Squares) Analysis
Statistical method assuming normally distributed, independent dimensional variations. Provides approximately 99.73% (3-sigma) assembly yield when all inputs are centered at nominal. Appropriate for production volumes above 500 units.

2.3 Monte Carlo Simulation
Numerical simulation using 10,000+ random samples drawn from actual or assumed distributions. Required for non-normal distributions, assemblies with more than 8 contributing dimensions, and cases where Cpk targets must be predicted. Ridgeline uses Crystal Ball or custom Python scripts for Monte Carlo analysis.

3. REQUIRED OUTPUTS
All tolerance analyses must produce:
- Dimension chain diagram (1D or 2D as appropriate)
- Nominal gap/interference calculation
- Worst-case min/max assembly dimensions
- Statistical assembly distribution (if RSS or Monte Carlo used)
- Predicted yield or Cpk at the assembly level
- Sensitivity ranking: which individual tolerances contribute most

4. GD&T COMPLIANCE
All tolerances referenced in the analysis must conform to ASME Y14.5-2018. Geometric tolerances (flatness, parallelism, position, etc.) must be converted to equivalent bilateral linear tolerances for the stack-up calculation using the methods described in the ASME Y14.5 standard and Bryan Fischer's 'Mechanical Tolerance Stackup and Analysis' (2nd edition, CRC Press, 2011).
